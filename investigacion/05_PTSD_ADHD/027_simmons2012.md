and “tbi” as search terms. These searches yielded 185, 168, and 193
articles, respectively, published online between 2000 and 2009.

3. Meta-analysis: activation likelihood estimation (ALE)
ALE maps were created as described by Turkeltaub et al. (2002)
using a full-width half-maximum (FWHM) of 10 mm. Statistical
signiﬁcance was determined using a permutation test of randomly
generated foci. No assumptions were made concerning the distri-
bution or spatial separation of these random foci. Five thousand
permutations were computed using the same FWHM value and the
same number of foci used in computing the ALE values. The test
was corrected for multiple comparisons using the false discovery
rate (FDR) method (Genovese et al., 2002; Laird et al., 2005). Due to
the disparity in available studies the minimum cluster volume
required for the TBI analysis set to 40 mm3 and 100 mm3 for the
PTSD dataset. All data processing was carried out using a Java
version of ginger Ale (www.brainmap.org). Individual maps were
created for the PTSD and mTBI analyses. Whole brain maps of the
ALE values were imported into MRIcron (www.sph.sc.edu/comd/
rorden/mricron/) for data visualization and overlaid onto an
anatomical template generated by spatially normalizing the Interna-
tional Consortium for Brain Mapping (ICBM) template to Talairach
space (Kochunov et al., 2002). To determine the overlap between
the clusters, the distances between foci from the PTSD and mTBI
analysis were calculated, including both absolute distance (d¼

4. Results
4.1. PTSD
As indicated in Table 1 and Fig. 1, the amygdala, the anterior
cingulate, and the middle frontal gyrus were identiﬁed across
studies as regions that were differentially activated between PTSD
and non-PTSD individuals. The bilateral amygdala/uncus was the
most frequently identiﬁed area of differential activation (Kemp et al.,
2007; Bryant et al., 2008, 2005; Williams et al., 2006; Protopopescu
et al., 2005; Felmingham et al., 2009; Werner et al., 2009; Thomaes
et al., 2009; Geuze et al., 2007; Yang et al., 2004; Shin et al., 2001,
2005; Rauch et al., 2000; New et al., 2009; Milad et al., 2009),
however most studies (Werner et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2004; New
et al., 2009) used a region of interest analysis, which may have
inﬂuenced the reported results. These studies predominantly used
trauma relevant stimuli, which is the primary reason that these
studies focused mainly on the amygdala (i.e., because in fMRI
studies, activation in smaller structures such as the amygdala, may
be obscured in a whole brain analysis due to the failure to obtain
a sufﬁcient cluster volume). Activation differences that were seen in
the middle frontal gyrus (Kim et al., 2008; Kemp et al., 2007; Werner
et al., 2009; Frewen et al., 2008; Lanius et al., 2007; Hou et al., 2007)
showed greater activation in controls versus PTSD groups. The
majority of these studies, implemented affective tasks requiring
exposure to and/or recall of trauma relevant stimuli. Interestingly,
these activations were split and nearly overlapping in parts of the
anterior cingulate, with adjacent regions showing greater activation
for PTSD patients and controls (see Fig.1). Several prior studies have
shown greater activation in PTSD individuals relative to healthy
controls (Bryant et al., 2005; Felmingham et al., 2009; Carrion et al.,
2008) as well as greater activation in immediately adjacent areas in
controls relative to PTSD individuals (Williams et al., 2006; Werner
et al., 2009; Bonne et al., 2008; Lanius et al., 2007).However, it is
important to note that the tasks that were used in the studies that
showed increased activation in PTSD individuals were largely
cognitive tasks (2 oddball and 1 go/no-go), whereas the tasks that
were used in the studies that showed increased activation in
controls were predominantly affective or fear processing tasks (2
memory, 1 face, and 1 script driven imagery). This highlights the
importance of task selection in functional imaging research. This
aspect is particularly important in PTSD research because avoidant
symptomology can have a strong effect on the resultant data
(Hopper et al., 2007; Strigo et al., 2010b).
4.2. mTBI
The meta-analysis results displayed in Table 2 and Fig. 2 point to
a widely distributed network of structures that may be affected by
mTBI. This is not surprising, given the paucity of fMRI research
comparing mTBI individuals with controls. Moreover, the studies
that have been conducted and were included in this meta-analysis
included a heterogeneous population (i.e., blast versus blunt force
injury) of individuals who performed a variety of different tasks.
With these caveats in mind, the results of this meta-analysis
suggest that mTBI is associated with dysregulated functional acti-
vation in several prefrontal, parietal and temporal regions, which
are involved in decision making and self-control. More speciﬁcally,
clusters in the superior and middle frontal gyri, superior and
inferior parietal lobules and superior temporal gyrus were differ-
entially activated between mTBI subjects and controls. Additionally,
mTBI versus control individuals also showed differential activation
in the medial frontal cortex, which is a central structure in the

4.3. Overlap
Foci in the two groups are contrasted in Table 3, where the
absolute foci distance and the distance removing laterality are
provided. When comparing the results of the PTSD and mTBI meta-
analyses, few regions were identiﬁed in both analyses. However,
overlap was noted in the middle frontal gyrus and to lesser extent
the caudate (TBI) and anterior cingulate (PTSD). The PTSD group
relative to controls showed greater activation to trauma relevant
traumatic material in the middle frontal gyrus (Kim et al., 2008;
Kemp et al., 2007; Werner et al., 2009; Frewen et al., 2008; Lanius
et al., 2007; Hou et al., 2007). While the middle frontal gyrus
showed ﬁve clusters of activation, only the primary activation for
the controls greater than the TBI group was right lateralized, and
this difference most evident during a the n-back task (McAllister
et al., 2001). While there were numerous methodological differ-
ences across studies, these results may provide an initial indication
of potential areas of overlap between individuals with PTSD and
individuals who develop neuropsychiatric symptoms after mTBI.
The caudate region from the TBI < control contrast that showed
overlap with the cingulate was powered by a study on a ﬁnger
tapping task (Wiese et al., 2006). Interestingly, the cingulate region
that was greater in PTSD than controls was primarily related to
cognitive tasks (Bryant et al., 2005; Felmingham et al., 2009;
Carrion et al., 2008), while the PTSD group deactivated this region
for memory (Werner et al., 2009; Bonne et al., 2008) or affective
(Williams et al., 2006; Lanius et al., 2007) tasks. This suggests that
this region may become stressed during more complex cognitive
processes and may be taken “off line” when distracted. As this
region was not previously identiﬁed as a region of potential overlap
