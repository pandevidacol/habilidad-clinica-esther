studies testing the effect of multi-week PA programs on clinical mental

two were conducted in the past ten years. The data were collected by 
research teams in 11 countries, representing [no less than] five academic

## Methodology
Methods: A systematic search of eight databases yielded 15 randomized controlled trial and 2 quasi-experimental 
design studies—including N = 881 youth (M = 9.75 years, 71% male)—that tested the effects of multi-week PA 
programs on symptoms and impairment of children with [or at-risk for] ADHD and/or ODD. 
Results: Random effects meta-analyses favored PA groups on omnibus ADHD measures (g = −0.42, 95%CI[-0.62;- 
0.21]), combined ADHD symptoms (g = −0.50, 95%CI[-0.82;-0.17]), inattention (g = −0.41,95%CI[-0.82; 
0.00]), and hyperactivity/impulsivity (g = −0.30, 95%CI[-0.56;-0.04]). Heterogeneity was moderate across 
studies (I2 = 49%, 95%CI[12%-to-70%]). Significant differences favored PA programs whether inclusion 
required diagnosis, programs augmented frontline treatments, and active or passive comparison groups were 
utilized.

## Clinical Significance
Conclusion: Diverse PA programs can reduce ADHD symptoms, especially where they intentionally pursue this 
end.   
An estimated 1 in 8 children in the United States meet criteria for 
Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity/Impulsivity Disorder (ADHD) or Oppo­
sitional Defiant Disorder (ODD) (Visser et al., 2016). ADHD is charac­
terized 
by 
a 
persistent 
pattern 
of 
inattention 
and/or 
hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with daily functioning and 
development (American Psychiatric Association (APA) Diagnostic and 
Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. 5th ed., 2013). Children with 
ADHD perform worse than typically developing (TD) peers on neuro­
psychological tests of executive function (Willcutt et al., 2005) and a 
recent meta-analysis of twin studies estimated that ADHD is heritable 
between 77% and 88% (Faraone & Larsson, 2019). However, ADHD is 
also thought to be heterogeneous such that rather than any single 
neurobiological etiology there are multiple neurodevelopmental 
pathways to impairment (Halperin et al., 2014). In contrast, ODD refers 
to defiant and disobedient behaviors that cause significant problems at 
home or school (American Psychiatric Association (APA) Diagnostic and 
Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. 5th ed., 2013). Children with ODD 
also evidence neurocognitive deficits compared to TD peers, but only in 
situations involving high motivational or affective aspects—what has 
been termed “hot executive function” (Noordermeer et al., 2016). ODD 
appears to be less heritable than ADHD, although still substantial 
(estimated in twin studies to be between 61% and 74%) (Lahey & 
Waldman, 2012). The two disorders are highly comorbid, such that 26% 
of children with ADHD have ODD, and 67% with ODD have ADHD 
(Visser et al., 2016).

However, it is estimated that only 40–65% of youth meeting criteria for 
ADHD or ODD receive these services (Gudi˜no et al., 2009; Merikangas

pairments that persist into adulthood, affecting long-term employment 
and health outcomes (Barkley, 2002). For these reasons, physical ac­
tivity (PA) has been identified as a promising adjunct intervention mo­
dality that is scalable, accessible, and capable of removing barriers to 
treatment and altering long-term neurodevelopmental trajectories 
(Halperin et al., 2012; Kazdin & Rabbitt, 2013). 
In 2019, a meta-analysis tested the effects of multi-week PA pro­
grams on cognition among TD children and children with ADHD. On the 
basis of significant benefits of PA programs across a range of neuro­
psychological tasks, and promising neurobiological mediators, the au­
thors proposed the first evidence-based recommendations for PA in 
children with ADHD (Christiansen et al., 2019). The following year, a 
video game was approved by the FDA as treatment for ADHD based upon 
its impact on the Test of Variables of Attention (TOVA)—a neuropsy­
chological test—in a randomized controlled trial (RCT), despite lack of 
indicated effects on symptoms or functional impairment (Kollins et al., 
2020). This focus on neuropsychological tests is reasonable: neuropsy­
chological tests are often valid, reliable, continuous, standardized, and 
modifiable with single bouts of PA. Neurocognitive measures are also 
the putative mediators by which PA affects clinical mental health out­
comes; and understanding them better supports causality and identifies 
levers for interventions to target (Halperin et al., 2012). 
However, making decisions based mainly upon neurobiological and 
cognitive outcomes in studies of PA and ADHD is potentially problem­
atic given that these are biomarker and surrogate response variables, 
respectively. Surrogate response variables are laboratory measures that 
are used in clinical trials as substitutes for the clinically relevant 
response (in this case, measure of daily functioning, such as symptoms 
and functional impairment). Surrogate response variables have proven 
problematic in situations where they did not have the presupposed 
relationship with the clinically relevant variable (Friedman et al., 2015). 
Problematic surrogate response variables in medicine have included LDL 
cholesterol for major cardiovascular events (Barter et al., 2007), CD-4 
lymphocyte level for AIDS incidence (Cohen, 1992), and tumor size 
for cancer mortality (Piedbois et al., 1992). Similar problems are 
possible in the PA-ADHD interaction. For example, an increase in 
cognitive performance arising from PA that is evident in a quiet room 
1-on-1 with a researcher may not translate to real-world environments 
characterized by high distraction and low motivation (Ramer et al., 
2020). 
To our knowledge, five meta-analyses have examined the effect of 
multi-week PA programs on ADHD and ODD clinical mental health 
outcomes (Cerrillo-Urbina et al., 2015; Cornelius et al., 2017; Seiffer 
et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Zang, 2019). The first of these, Cerril­
lo-Urbina (2015), reported significant moderate-large effects on inat­
tention and hyperactivity/impulsivity based on a small handful of 
studies 
(Cerrillo-Urbina 
et

2015). 
The 
subsequent 
two 
meta-analyses, were larger, reported non-significant findings, and suf­
fered from methodological and/or reporting problems (Cornelius et al., 
2017; Zang, 2019). In 2021, two additional meta-analyses were pub­
lished. The first included eleven RCTs and reported moderate significant 
effects of multi-week PA programs on ADHD combined symptoms, 
inattention symptoms, and functional impairment, but not hyper­
active/impulsive symptoms (Seiffer et al., 2021). Authors also reported 
that PA programs were only effective when used as an adjunct to existing 
treatments and when compared to passive comparison groups (Seiffer 
et al., 2021). The second, reported large significant reductions in inat­
tention symptoms based on eight studies but near-zero effects on 
hyperactive/impulsive symptoms based on six studies (Xie et al., 2021). 
In sum, estimates of the effect of PA programs on ADHD symptoms have 
varied widely, attempts to investigate these relationships have suffered 
methodological limitations, and there remains limited evidence on 
moderators, functional impairment, and ODD symptoms. 
Therefore, we conducted this meta-analysis to evaluate the effect of 
multi-week PA programs on ADHD and ODD clinical mental health 
outcomes. We build on previous meta-analyses by focusing on published 
methodologically strong (i.e., meeting What Works Clearinghouse 
[WWC] standards for strong evidence) RCT and quasi-experimental 
design (QED) studies testing multi-week PA programs, testing effects 
on ODD symptoms and functional impairment, using conservative sta­
tistical analyses, conducting sensitivity analyses, and assessing study 
quality. We also examine potential moderation by study quality, com­
parison group, implementation modality, and inclusion criteria. 
1. Method 
This meta-analysis was conducted and reported according to the 
Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses 
(PRISMA) (Moher et al., 2015). The protocol is registered with PROS­
PERO (CRD42017076864). The study was determined by the University 
of Illinois Chicago Institutional Review Board to not represent human 
subjects research. Template data, collection forms, data extracted from 
included studies; data used for all analyses, analytic code, and any other 
materials used in the review are available upon request. 
1.1. Data sources and searches 
A comprehensive search strategy was designed to identify all RCT 
and QED studies testing the effects of multi-week PA programs against a 
comparison group on ADHD and ODD clinical mental health outcomes. 
Relevant controlled vocabulary and keywords probed three concepts: 
(1) ADHD and disruptive behavior disorders, (2) children 18 years of age 
and younger, and (3) physical activity. Searches were conducted on 
August 23rd, 2017, July 17th-20th 2020, and March 16th, 2022 in the 
following databases: PubMed, EMBASE, CINAHL, ERIC, Cochrane Li­
brary, PsycINFO, Clinicaltrials.gov, and NIH Reporter. Results were 
limited to publications from 1987 to present, 1987 being the year that 
the American Psychological Association (APA) introduced the term 
ADHD. Studies in English, Spanish, or Portuguese languages were 
included in the search. Table 1 provides an overview of the strategy with 
examples of the keywords used; the full strategy for PubMed, including 
subject headings, is provided online in Appendix A (Table A1).

1.2. Study selection 
Studies found after the search were compiled into an EndNote X8 
library. Titles and abstracts of records were screened independently by 
three review authors (JDR, MESR, GMB), who removed duplicates and 
determined whether additional consideration of the studies was war­
ranted. Each review author (JDR, MESR, GMB) independently reviewed 
titles, abstracts, and full texts of the studies to assess eligibility. Any 
disagreements were discussed for consensus with a final decision for 
inclusion or exclusion made by a fourth author (EEB). At this point, ci­
tations from eligible studies and previous systematic reviews were 
searched to identify additional studies, these were reviewed and 
screened as described above. 
Studies published in peer-reviewed journals that met the following 
criteria were eligible for inclusion: (1) strong RCT or QED, where strong 
was defined as meeting What Works Clearinghouse (WWC) standards (i. 
e., acceptable level of attrition, similar attrition in each group, evidence 
of baseline equivalence, free of confounding factors; see Figure II1 in the 
WWC Standards Handbook Version 4.0); (2) compare a multi-week PA 
program to a comparison condition on ADHD or ODD clinical mental 
health outcomes (i.e., parent or teacher-reported symptoms and func­
tional impairment using validated instruments or direct observation); 
(3) a sample of children 3-18 years-old, diagnosed with [or with 
elevated symptoms of] ADHD and/or ODD; and (4) provide sufficient 
information to compute effect size. 
1.3. Data extraction and assessment of study quality 
Data extraction was conducted independently by three review au­
thors (JDR, MESR, GMB) using standardized data abstraction forms. 
Extracted information included: author, year, study design, sample size, 
participant characteristics, intervention characteristics, intervention 
adherence, comparison group characteristics, comparison group 
adherence, study retention, study outcomes, measurement time points, 
statistical methods, statistical results, and study quality. Study quality 
was assessed using the Study Design and Implementation Assessment 
Device (DIAD) (Valentine & Cooper, 2008). The DIAD focuses on the 
design and operational details of studies and results in four profile scores 
that range from zero to three (0 = no, 1 = maybe no, 2 = maybe yes, 3 =
yes). Profile scores assess Clarity of Causal Inference (i.e., internal val­
idity), Generality of Findings (i.e., external validity), Precision of 
Outcome Estimation (i.e., statistical validity), and Fit between Concepts 
and Operations (i.e., construct validity). Operational definitions and 
decision rules were specified to enhance reliability of coding. Dis­
agreements between the three review authors were discussed for 
consensus with a final decision made by a fourth author (blinded for 
review). Percent agreement between the three authors was assessed on 
three studies prior to independent extraction. On average, data extrac­
tors agreed on 85% of codes (Study 1 = 78%, Study 2 = 86%, and Study 
3 = 90%). 
1.4. Data synthesis and analysis 
Effect sizes for each study were calculated as standardized mean 
differences (SMD) on the outcome, with adjustment for small sample 
bias (Hedges’ g). Effect sizes, where possible, were computed from 
means and standard deviations on outcome measures that were included 
in the study report or made available by study authors. When available, 
pretest means were subtracted from the posttest means to adjust for 
potential differences between treatment and comparison groups at 
baseline and thus enhance precision in the estimation of effect sizes 
(Lipsey & Wilson, 2000). Alternatively, when necessary, effect sizes 
were estimated from relevant test statistics or their reported exact sig­
nificance levels (Rosenthal et al., 1994). In the case of one study 
(Bowling et al., 2017), measures were dichotomized and reported as 
odds ratios; these odds ratios were converted to SMD using the Cox index 
(S´anchez-Meca et al., 2003). Effect sizes that were more than three 
interquartile ranges above the 75th percentile or below the 25th 
percentile, and thus statistical outliers (Tukey, 1977), were winsorized 
by setting their values to the highest or lowest effect size that did not 
qualify as an outlier. One study had its effect size winsorized (Ghahra­
mani et al., 2016). 
Given the likelihood of variability of effect by PA program type and 
quality, as well as potentially by sample characteristics, quantitative 
syntheses were conducted using random-effects models (Hedges & 
Vevea, 1998). When more than one measure was available for a given 
type of outcome (e.g., ADHD inattentive symptoms) in a study, the 
corresponding effect sizes were averaged to yield a single effect size for 
that type of outcome for the study. Effect sizes were synthesized using 
restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation, with each effect size 
weighted by the inverse of its sampling variances (which correlates 
strongly with study sample size) (Raudenbush, 2009). Overall weighted 
mean effect sizes and 95% confidence intervals were calculated across 
studies for all outcomes with at least two effect sizes. These included 
omnibus ADHD measures, inattentive symptoms, hyperactive/impulsive 
symptoms, combined ADHD symptoms, ODD symptoms, and functional 
impairment. Omnibus ADHD measures included any parent or teacher 
report measures of inattention, hyperactivity-impulsivity, or ADHD 
combined symptoms, with averaging across effect sizes for multiple 
measures when available for a study (e.g., teacher ratings of inattention, 
parent ratings of hyperactivity) as well as systematic observations of 
disruptive behaviors. Heterogeneity in effect sizes across studies was 
measured with the I-squared statistic (Higgins et al., 2003) and predic­
tion intervals were calculated and included in forest plots (Higgins et al., 
2019). 
Based upon a priori hypotheses, meta-regression analyses were con­
ducted to test moderation of effects by type of comparison group (i.e., 
active vs. passive), implementation modality (stand-alone vs. adjunct to 
front-line treatment), and study quality (operationalized as the sum of 
the four profile scores of the DIAD). We performed leave-one-out ana­
lyses in which each effect size was sequentially removed to determine 
whether doing so significantly altered the summary effect size on 
omnibus ADHD measures. We also conducted a sensitivity analysis on 
omnibus ADHD measures that excluded one study that slightly sur­
passed the differential attrition rate suggested by WWC (Choi et al., 
2015) and five studies that did not report attrition, drop-out, missing 
data, provide a flow diagram, or respond to e-mail’s requesting infor­
mation to estimate attrition (Abadi et al., 2008; Ahmed & Mohamed, 
2011; García-G´omez et al., 2016; Ghahramani et al., 2016; Verret et al., 
2012). Publication bias was assessed by visual inspection of funnel plots 
and Egger’s test of the intercept (Egger et al., 1997). Models, funnel 
plots, and forest plots were generated with the RStudio dmetar package 
(Harrer et al., 2021).
