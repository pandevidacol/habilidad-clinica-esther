## Methodology
MATERIALS AND METHODS 
92
 
Participants 
93
 
Participants were 31 physically healthy Bar-Ilan University students (74.2% women), 
94
 
who were recruited through ads placed around the university campus and on the 
95
 
internet. The sample's average age was 24.13 (SD = 4.27, range 18–42), and their 
96
 
average years of education was 15.6 (SD = 2.15, range 12–19). Fourteen participants 
97
 
had a formal diagnosis of ADHD, given by their neurologist or psychiatrist, and 17 
98
 
participants served as controls, with no history of psychiatric or neurological 
99
 
diagnoses. Mean age of ADHD diagnosis was 15.7 (SD = 4.39), with 42% 
100
 
hyperactive type. Most ADHD participants (71%) had prescriptions for a stimulant 
101
 
medication (Ritalin, Concerta, or Adderall). Upon arriving at the lab, participants 
102
 
received a full explanation of the research procedures from a trained research 
103
 
assistant. All participants then gave a written informed consent according to 
104
 
procedures approved by the Bar-Ilan University Institutional Review Board. 
105
 
 
106
 
Measures and procedure 
107
 
All participants completed the Conners Continuous Auditory Test of Attention 
108
 
(CATA), administered on a Dell Pentium computer, driven at 160 Hz. The CATA is a 
109
 
standardized continuous performance test, with negligible practice effects (Greenberg, 
110
 
1987). It is administered in the auditory modality through earphones, thereby 
111
 
minimizing distractibility that may be difficult to overcome when exercising using 
112
 
visual tasks. The CATA task assesses auditory processing and attention-related 
113
 
problems in individuals aged 8 years and older. The task has 200 scored trials, divided 
114
 
into 4 blocks of 50 trials. Within each block, 80% of the trials are warned trials, on 
115
 
which a low tone (warning) is followed by a high tone (target). The remaining 20% of 
116
 
the trials are unwarned, in which the high tone is played without warning (non-target). 
117
 
Participants are asked to respond by left-clicking the mouse with the index finger of 
118
 
their dominant hand as fast as they can to high tones on warned trials but ignore high 
119
 
tones on unwarned trials.  
120
 
 
121
 
The measures used in the present analyses are described in detail elsewhere 
122
 
(Kallo & Rassovsky, 2017). Below is a summary of these measures: 
123
 
1. Detectability (DPR) is a measure of the respondent’s ability to discriminate targets 
124
 
(i.e., the high-tone sound on warned trials) from non-targets (i.e., the high-tone sound 
125
 
on unwarned trials), with higher raw and T score value indicating worse performance 
126
 
(i.e., poorer discrimination).  
127
 
2. Hit Reaction Time (HRT) is the mean response speed, measured in milliseconds, 
128
 
for all non-perseverative target responses made during the entire administration.  
129
 
3. Omissions (OMI) are missed targets, indexed by the percent of non-responses to 
130
 
targets.  
131
 
4. Commissions (COM) are incorrect responses to non-targets, indexed by the percent 
132
 
of responses to non-targets.  
133
 
5. C is a signal detection statistic that measures an individual’s natural response style 
134
 
and can be classified as having a conservative style that emphasizes accuracy over 
135
 
speed, a liberal style that emphasizes speed over accuracy, or a balanced style that is 
136
 
biased neither toward speed nor toward accuracy. Higher T scores represent more 
137
 
conservative response style.  
138
 
 
139
 
The test was administered twice, at baseline in a sitting position and while 
140
 
walking on a York treadmill (model Capital25) at a speed of 5 km/h, both closely

Data analysis 
149
 
Due to significant age difference between the ADHD and control groups, exploratory 
150
 
analyses for identification of outliers were conducted for age-matching. These 
151
 
analyses identified one 42-year-old participant in the ADHD sample, who was 
152
 
excluded from subsequent analyses. Potential age and gender differences between the 
153
 
groups were examined using independent samples t-test and Chi-square test, 
154
 
respectively. A 2 × 2 analysis of variance (ANOVA) with repeated measures was used 
155
 
to investigate the effects of exercise on attention, followed by post-hoc analyses with 
156
 
Bonferroni correction to decompose the two-way interactions. Exercise was the 
157
 
within-subjects variable and ADHD diagnosis was the between-subjects variable. All 
158
 
the aforementioned measures generated by the CATA were converted to T-scores and 
159
 
entered as dependent variables.

INTRODUCTION 
45
 
Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is characterized by difficulties 
46
 
paying and/or sustaining attention, often accompanied by deficits in controlling or 
47
 
inhibiting behavior (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). In efforts to explain the 
48
 
variability in the different and complex problems exhibited by individuals with 
49
 
ADHD, contemporary leading theories have identified the core of the pathology with 
50
 
a deficit in attentional and executive functions, such as sustained and divided 
51
 
attention, attentional and inhibitory control, working memory, planning, cognitive 
52
 
flexibility, and problem solving (Barkley, 1997; Diamond, 2013; Petersen & Posner, 
53
 
2012). Neuropsychological assessments of these deficits typically include, among 
54
 
others, continuous performance tasks (CPTs), which assess the various aspects of 
55
 
these attentional processes. Studies employing these measures report a clear pattern of 
56
 
performance distinguishing adults with ADHD and healthy adults, such that ADHD 
57
 
participants demonstrate significantly more omission and commission errors and 
58
 
lower sensitivity (Riccio et al., 2001).  
59
 
 
60
 
Accumulating data suggest a positive effect of physical activity on cognitive 
61
 
functioning in ADHD. These positive effects are supported both by animal and human 
62
 
studies. Robinson, Hopkins, and Bucci (2011), for example, studied spontaneously 
63
 
hyperactive adolescent rats and found that engagement in physical exercise resulted in 
64
 
subjects’ reduced distractibility and improved attentional capabilities. Studies in 
65
 
humans reported similar findings. Pontifex et al. (2013), for example, showed that 
66
 
both children with ADHD and a matched, healthy control group exhibited improved 
67
 
cognitive performance on measures of executive and attentional functions, as well as 
68
 
reduced ADHD symptoms, following moderate physical activity. Gapin and Etnier 
69
 
(2010) found that more frequent physical activity was associated with better executive 
70
 
functioning, including inhibition, planning, working memory, and processing speed, 
71
 
in children with ADHD. Chang et al. (2012) found that moderate to intense aerobic 
72
 
exercise facilitated inhibition and set shifting, functions that characterize the primary 
73
 
executive deficits in ADHD.  
74
 
 
75
 
Most prior studies examined the effects of physical activity on attention by 
76
 
assessing attentional performance before and immediately following physical training 
77
 
Lambourne & Tomporowski (2010). To expand on this literature, the present pilot 
78
 
study aimed to examine the influence of physical exercise conducted during 
79
 
performance of an attentional task. To this end, individuals with ADHD and a 
80
 
normative control sample engaged in physical activity, while simultaneously 
81
 
performing an auditory CPT. We capitalized on previous efforts to explain the 
82
 
mechanism underlying ADHD-related deficits as an imbalance in the 
83
 
catecholaminergic systems in the brain, leading to dysfunction of attentional circuits 
84
 
(Yu-Kai, Suyen, Hui-Hsiang, & Yuan-Hung Lee, 2012). Specifically, it has been 
85
 
hypothesized that attentional impairments may be due to a hypodopaminergic state in 
86
 
the prefrontal cortex, as supported by the common use of stimulant medications in the 
87
 
treatment of ADHD (Solanto, 2002). Therefore, given the potentially lower arousal 
88
 
levels among individuals with ADHD, we predicted a greater benefit to emerge 
89
 
among individuals with ADHD, as compared to healthy controls.

## Clinical Significance
conclusions are limited by the 
252
 
relatively small sample size. Another limitation of this study is that all participants 
253
 
exercised at the same intensity level, without controlling for several important 
254
 
physiological parameters, such as aerobic fitness, heart rate, and BMI, which could 
255
 
have moderated individual responses. Nonetheless, significant interaction effects, 
256
 
with moderate effect sizes, were found on several key measures, suggesting a 
257
 
potential benefit of physical activity to attentional processes in adult ADHD. It adds 
258
 
to the growing body of research supporting the beneficial effects of exercise on 
259
 
cognitive functions. Together, these studies suggest that, in addition to the myriad of 
260
 
physical and cognitive benefits that can be derived from regular exercises, physical 
261
 
activity while performing attentional tasks may bolster cognitive performance in 
262
 
individuals with ADHD. Upon replication in larger samples, these findings could be 
263
 
readily translated to practical applications in the classroom or working environment. 
264
 
Pedaling on a stationary bicycle or walking on a treadmill during lecture or work may 
265
 
prove beneficial for both physical and brain health.
