Individual CBT programs
Rostain and Ramsay examined the outcome of a combination of 
pharmacotherapy and individual CBT following McDermott’s 
adaptation of cognitive therapy for adult ADHD [30,31]. Their 
preliminary open study of 43 patients suggested improve-
ments on measures of ADHD, comorbid symptoms and overall 
­functioning [15].
The cognitive behavioral intervention presented by Safren et al. 
in 2005 comprises several modules [16]: module 1 includes psycho­
education and organizational assistance such as the introduction 
of a calendar system; module 2 provides coping strategies for 
distractability, while module 3 comprises elements of cognitive 
restructuring. Additional optional modules include management 
of the inclination to procrastinate, improvement of communica-
tion skills as well as the control of anger. Patients were eligible 
for this controlled study if they still suffered from residual symp-
toms of ADHD after at least 2 months of medication (mainly 
stimulants, bupropion and venlafaxine) and demonstrated at least 
moderate impairment in their level of functioning (clinical global 
impression ≥4 on a scale from 1 to 7). Patients in the intervention 
condition participated in at least ten individual therapy sessions 
over 15 weeks, which was compared with a control group who 
received medication only. Subsequent to completion of treatment, 
the authors reported an increase in the general level of functioning 
(as measured by a 2-point increase in the clinical global impres-
sion) of 56% in the combination therapy group versus 13% in 
the medication-only group. The strongest effects were found in 
ADHD symptomatology, as well as in depression and anxiety. 
However, the number of individual therapy sessions conducted on 
average for each patient was not reported, nor which psychothera-
peutic contents were conveyed in the optional modules. Another 
controlled randomized study published by Safren in 2010 com-
pared CBT to relaxation with educational support. Medication-
treated adults with persistent ADHD symptoms (n = 86) were 
randomized to 12 individual sessions. CBT was found to signifi-
cantly reduce ADHD symptomatology (response: 67 vs 33%), 
with effects maintained for 1 year post-therapy [17].

During the last decade, the efficacy of psychotherapeutic inter-
ventions for ADHD in adults increasingly became a subject of 
scientific research. However, only cognitive behavioral ­therapy 
(CBT)-oriented concepts have been evaluated to date (Tables 1 & 2).
Evaluated psychotherapeutic programs
In 1999, the first study investigating a disorder-oriented psychoe-
ducational program was published [10]. The 4-week group treat-
ment comprised education on the symptomatology of ADHD 
as well as organizational assistance. Subsequent to participa-
tion, a subjective improvement in ADHD symptoms was found. 
However, the intervention proved to have unfavorable effects on 
patients’ self-esteem. The reason for the negative influence of this 
4-week psychoeducation on self-esteem could be that participants 
were not able, in this time, to sufficiently activate inner resources 
and to acquire skills to cope with their problems.
Subsequently, the program was extended to 10 weeks of therapy 
including training on specific skills and coping strategies, but 
the outcomes of the program have not yet been published [11]. 
Prospective studies published since 2002 demonstrate positive 
effects of cognitive and dialectic behavioral therapy approaches 
in groups as well as individual therapy [12–22]. In these studies, 
patients demonstrate a reduction in both the severity of ADHD as 
well as frequently associated symptoms such as depression, anxi-
ety, lack of self-esteem and anger. Furthermore, a self-directed 
support program [23], mindfulness meditation training in a group 
setting [24] and a meta-cognitive group therapy program [18,25] 
have been evaluated. The majority of these approaches work on 
a manualized and/or modular basis and address multiple areas 
of concern. As a general rule, a psychoeducational introduction 
is followed by modules covering subjects such as self-monitoring, 
mindfulness, emotion regulation, dysfunctional cognitions, time 
management and organization. Interestingly, for adults with 
ADHD, group therapy programs have been evaluated more 
frequently than individual therapy, and all those studied have 
been well structured and have showed positive effects. Moreover, 
participants report benefitting greatly from the interpersonal 
exchange permitted by the group setting [12,14,25,26]. The fol-
lowing sections provide a concise review of evaluated therapy 
programs.
Cognitive behavioral therapy
The early development of many ADHD patients is characterized 
by unfavorable learning experiences. Impairments in attentional 
performance and concentration, as well as in interactive behavior 
during childhood (often affected by hyperactivity, impulsivity 
and distractibility), may lead to rejection of the patient by the 
peer group [27,28], as well as by significant others. Repetitive nega-
tive feedback promotes the development of dysfunctional behav-
ioral patterns, deep-rooted convictions and presumptions mainly 
about oneself but also about others. Consequently, a vicious cir-
cle involving thoughts and emotions may arise, affecting the 
patient’s motivation. Thus, many patients do not exhaust their 
options and remain below their potential. Dysfunctional cogni-
tions revolving mostly around failure (“Actually, I can do it, but 
I am too lazy!”, “as usual, I won’t make it anyway”) give rise to 
depression, anxiety and discontent but also to feelings of guilt. 
Often, these thought patterns aggravate the underlying ADHD 
symptomatology [14,16].
The objective of cognitive behavioral strategies in adult ADHD 
patients is to identify and modify dysfunctional cognitions and 
behavioral patterns, as well as to create awareness of present sus-
ceptibility factors. In behavioral experiments and with the help

Group CBT programs
A comprehensive CBT intervention was developed by Young and 
Bramham at Maudsley Hospital (London, UK, Young–Bramham 
Program) [34]. The program was evaluated in a brief intensive 
group format comparing 61 stably medicated participants with 
a medication-only waitlist group [12]. The psychotherapeutic 
treatment consisted of six workshops, which were conducted dur-
ing three whole days (1 day/month) and included 10–15 patients 
per workshop. Subsequent to treatment, the intervention group 
demonstrated significant enhancement of knowledge about 
ADHD as well as improvement in self-esteem and self-efficacy 
compared with the control group. Both groups showed a reduc-
tion in anxiety and depression. Consistent with other reports 
[14,26], the participants found the exchange of experiences with 
other patients particularly helpful. The contents of the program 
have been made available in the form of a book in both English 
and Spanish [34,35].
Likewise, the effect of a cognitive behaviorally oriented psy-
chological group rehabilitation has recently been investigated 
in Finland in an open study (n = 29) with ten weekly sessions 
[20,36]. Comparable with the study on short-term individual CBT, 
the program resulted in reduced self-rated ADHD symptoms 
(assessed on the Brown Attention-Deficit Disorder Scale) [32] 
and commonly associated symptoms (assessed with the SCL-16 
­questionnaire) [13].

Cognitive remediation program
In Australia, Stevenson et al. reported a symptom-oriented 8-week 
manualized group therapy program based on the principles of 
CBT and coaching. The program aimed to improve organizational 
skills, management of impulsivity and anger, and to enhance self-
esteem [19]. In order to provide assistance with questions that arose 
during homework completed between structured sessions, stu-
dents or support persons chosen by patients were appointed who 
contacted patients by phone.
The efficacy of this program was studied in a randomized con-
trolled trial with 43 participants, of whom 50% were stably medi-
cated. Immediately after completion of the program, all 22 patients 
who had participated in the group program demonstrated a sig-
nificant improvement in all investigated symptoms compared with 
the control group (severity of ADHD, organizational skills, self-
esteem, extent and control of anger). The improvements in severity 
of ADHD, organizational skills and self-esteem were maintained 
at follow-up assessments conducted after 2 and 12 months.
Meta-cognitive training
Apart from classical CBT elements, the development of a 
­meta-cognitive treatment program began in 1999 [37], an open 
(uncontrolled) trial was published in 2008 [18] and a randomized 
controlled trial was published in 2010 [25]. Meta-cognitive group 
training aims to improve executive functions and self-manage-
ment, assisting patients to generate and eventually automate 
more functional behavioral patterns. Particular importance is 
attached to time management and structuring of everyday life. An 
open observational study with 38 mostly medicated participants 
showed significant improvement by self-assessment in inattentive-
ness, motivation, time management and organization of everyday 
life after completion of an 8- or 12-week program. No changes 
were observed for hyperactivity and impulsivity [18].
Solanto et al. [25] showed that group supportive therapy alone 
was inferior to meta-cognitive therapy in reducing inattention 
and disorganization in adult ADHD patients. In this controlled 
trial (n = 88), the efficacy of the meta-cognitive group train-
ing was compared with group supportive therapy, to control for 
nonspecific elements of the meta-cognitive therapy program. 
The study population consisted mainly of women (71% meta-
cognitive group; 61% control group) of comparatively high IQ 
(IQ 119 meta-cognitive group; IQ 114 control group), who were 
mostly of the inattentive subtype (69% meta-cognitive group; 
65% control group). The results clearly demonstrated the benefit 
of meta-cognitive training over supportive therapy with regard 
to the reduction of ADHD symptoms of inattention, time man-
agement, organization and planning. Female participants, and 
those of the inattentive subtype, were more likely to complete the 
­meta-cognitive program.
Reasoning & rehabilitation
Recently, Young et al. in Iceland led by Emilsson et al. [38] tested a 
15-session CBT-based group treatment for ADHD and comorbid 
problems in comparison with treatment as usual. The treatment, 
originally developed in 2007 for youths with ADHD and antisocial 
behavior, is based on the reasoning and rehabilitation program 
(R&R2), a prosocial competence training program for correctional 
settings. Participants in the CBT program met for group sessions 
twice weekly. In addition to the group program, coaches met with 
the group participants once per week. Using an add-on study design 
as first employed by Safren et al. [16], the study aimed to evaluate 
potential additive effects of this newly developed program R&R2 
in medicated adult ADHD patients, but in a group setting. The 
group program resulted in significant decreases in ADHD symp-
toms and antisocial behavior compared with treatment as usual. 
At 3-month follow-up, commonly associated problems (anxiety, 
depression, emotional control) and social functioning were also 
found to have significantly improved. Use of concomitant therapies 
during the treatment and follow-up period were not systematically 
assessed. Recently, the R&R2 ADHD group intervention was also 
evaluated among personality-­disordered offenders with ADHD 
symptoms and a waitlist control [39].
Dialectical behavioral therapy
The group therapy program developed in Freiburg at the end of 
the 1990s also combines psychoeducation and cognitive behav-
ioral interventions. It is based on the elements of the dialectical 
behavioral therapy [40] due to the overlapping clinical features 
of adult ADHD and borderline personality disorder [41–44]. In 
a pilot study [13], and in an open multicenter feasibility study 
with 72 participants [14], significant effects on ADHD symp-
toms, depression and general well-being were demonstrated, and 
the program was easily adapted into clinical daily routine. The 
group setting was highly appreciated by the participants, who 
rated behavior analysis, mindfulness and emotion regulation as 
the most ­helpful elements.
