Attendance and retention were excellent, and high
satisfaction ratings suggested that participants liked the
integrated intervention content and the group format. A
preliminary exploration of outcome data indicated that
emerging adults with elevated ADHD symptoms reported
lower levels of inattention and hyperactivity/impulsivity,
while participants with and without elevated ADHD
symptoms reported a reduction in relationship negativity.
Partner ratings corroborated some, but not all, areas of
improvement. In sum, results from this open trial
highlight the feasibility, acceptability, and potential utility
of the integrative group program for emerging adults with
clinically elevated ADHD symptoms.
To have 15 couples attend the integrated group
intervention without any dropouts underscores the feasi-
bility and acceptability of this program. Moreover, it was
equally impressive that an inexpensive recruitment strategy
(i.e., posting flyers on bus stops and Craigslist.com)
advertising a couples group for individuals with ADHD
resulted in over 140 callers with interest in the program.
That said, the fact that only slightly more than 10% of
individuals who called with interest in the integrated
intervention ended up completing it underscores the
need to consider how we can improve recruitment and
treatment delivery. For example, a number of callers were
unable to meet the weeknights the group was scheduled to
occur. Given that many young couples do not have much
shared time in early evening hours due to school or work
obligations, offering the program during lunch hours, on
weekends, or online with video chat technology are
intriguing alternatives. A common reason for folks being
excluded from this trial was having children. As emerging
adults with ADHD tend to get pregnant at younger ages
than emerging adults without ADHD (Flory, Molina,
Pelham, Gnagy, & Smith, 2006), we are planning to adapt
the integrated intervention to address concerns faced by
young parents. Finally, in light of a number of interested
callers having a difficult time convincing their partner to
join the group, future recruitment efforts will include
attempts made to contact their partners directly in order to
have a conversation with them about potential barriers and
treatment expectations with hopes that will improve rates of
partner engagement.
Participant attendance and treatment satisfaction
ratings support the palatability of the integrative group
program for emerging adults with elevated ADHD
symptoms. For a population characterized by its forget-
fulness and inconsistent motivation, especially at this
age, it was striking that no couples dropped out of
treatment, that both members of most (73%) couples
attended every session, and that all but one couple
arrived on time or early for at least half of the sessions.
Though our homework compliance results were less
impressive, rates of homework completion were similar
to those reported by clinical trials for other psychopa-
thologies (e.g., substance use; Carroll, Nich, & Ball,
2005). Treatment adherence and acceptability data are
limited in studies of psychosocial treatment for adult
ADHD. Virta and colleagues (2008) indicated very few
treatment dropouts (1 out of 30) and good attendance
(participants attended 86% of sessions) for their CBT
program. Solanto et al. (2008) reported eight dropouts
(79% sample retained), with 50% of treatment com-
pleters attending every session, 40% missing one or 2
sessions, and the remainder missing three or more.
However, neither study reported the level of engage-
ment (e.g., timeliness, homework completion) or par-
ticipant satisfaction with their programs. Three
randomized clinical trials (Safren et al., 2010; Solanto
et al., 2010; Weiss et al., 2012) also reported low attrition,
but no study indicated the level of participant atten-
dance, engagement, or treatment satisfaction. Given the
dearth of adherence and acceptability data reported by
prior studies of CBT for adult ADHD, it is difficult to
assess the relative significance of our findings. Still, our
results underscore the ability of the integrative group
program to engage emerging adults with clinically
elevated ADHD symptoms and their partners.

Though caution should be taken when interpreting
treatment effects observed in pilot studies (Leon et al.,
2011), effects of the integrative group program on ADHD
symptoms and relationship impairment are worth discuss-
ing briefly. The participants with elevated ADHD symptoms
reported lower levels of inattention and hyperactivity/
impulsivity after the integrative group program. The
positive effects of treatment on self-reported ADHD
symptoms, particularly hyperactivity/impulsivity, are com-
parable to effects reported in prior open trials of CBT for
adult ADHD (e.g., Solanto et al., 2008; Virta et al., 2008).
The effect size similarity is especially noteworthy when
considering that the integrative group program was much
briefer (6 sessions x 90 min/session = 540 min) than
therapies tested in the other open trials (800–1440 min).
However, longer CBT programs for adult ADHD tested in
randomized clinical trials (e.g., Safren et al., 2010; Solanto
et al., 2010) found larger effects on self-reported ADHD
symptoms than those found in this study. Further refine-
ment of the integrative group intervention (e.g., including
more content for inattentiveness) may be necessary to
improve its impact on ADHD symptoms.
After treatment, participants reported less relation-
ship negativity, and partners reported that their
significant others exhibited fewer conflict-inducing
behaviors. While preliminary, this is one of a small
group of studies to find positive effects of treatment in a
core domain of functional impairment for individuals
with elevated ADHD symptoms (for others, see Barkley
& Cox, 2007; Rostain & Ramsay, 2006; Weiss et al.,
2012). Acknowledging the deleterious impact of ADHD
symptoms on adult romantic relationships (e.g., Bieder-
man et al., 2006; Canu, Tabor, Michael, Bazzini, &
Elmore, 2014; Kessler et al., 2006; Wymbs et al., 2012),
finding effects of the integrative group program on
romantic relationships is encouraging. Improvement in
relationship functioning may be valuable because it could
facilitate romantic partners continuing to compensate for
the difficulties of adults with elevated ADHD symptoms
(Eakin et al., 2004). Of course, finding a change in
relationship functioning was not surprising given that
treatment included evidence-based, relationship-building
skills (Baucom et al., 2012). Future studies are needed to
investigate whether the integrative intervention facilitates
improvement in other areas of functioning impaired in
emerging adults with ADHD (e.g., managing finances,
substance use).
Interestingly, we found that partners corroborated
many, but not all, areas of improvement reported by the
participants with elevated ADHD symptoms. For example,
partners agreed that males with elevated ADHD symptoms
exhibited fewer symptoms of hyperactivity/impulsivity after
treatment. Conversely, partners of females with elevated
ADHD symptoms did not corroborate their self-reported
improvement in hyperactivity/impulsivity. Although many
studies of CBT for adult ADHD have included clinician
assessments of functioning, only two studies have relied on
assessments from significant others. Virta et al. (2008) found
that significant others failed to notice improvement in
ADHD symptoms that adults with ADHD reported. In
contrast, Solanto and colleagues (2010) found that
significant others corroborated posttreatment gains
reported by adults with ADHD. Given the somewhat
unreliable self-perceptions of adults with ADHD (e.g.,
Knouse et al., 2005; Zucker et al., 2002), gathering evidence
from significant others is important to judge observable
effects of treatment.
Though preliminary data for the integrative group
program are encouraging, several factors limit generaliz-
ability of these findings. First and foremost, the lack of a
control group is an important limitation of this study.
There is reason to believe that recruitment feasibility and
participant satisfaction ratings may have differed without
a control condition or random assignment to condition
(Leon et al., 2011). It also makes it impossible to conclude
that preliminary symptom change findings were direct
result of treatment (rather than from the passage of
time). Future studies will need to compare feasibility,
acceptability, and outcome data of participants in the
integrative protocol group versus those in a waitlist group
or an active treatment control group (e.g., self-help,
stimulant medication). Second, the small sample size
limited the analysis to examination of effect sizes, and the
limited sample size resulted in wide-ranging confidence
intervals for many of these effects. As such, readers should
take caution before overinterpreting these results, partic-
ularly those of gender-specific comparisons. Larger trials
of this program will allow for more confidence to be
placed in effect sizes as well as for traditional significance
tests to be conducted. Third, no follow-up data were
collected. In order to justify the efficacy of the integrative
group treatment, investigators will need to show long-
term maintenance of gains beyond assessments conduct-
ed immediately after treatment. Fourth, participants with
elevated ADHD symptoms were not required to have
ADHD diagnoses or receive treatment for ADHD as
children, though many did. As such, like many treatment
outcome studies to this point (e.g., Solanto et al., 2008;
Virta et al., 2008), the generalizability of results discussed
herein may only extend to the population sampled. Fifth,
couples with children were excluded. Additional sessions
that include parent management training may be needed
for couples with children. Future studies may also benefit
from incorporating enhanced recruitment procedures to
engage more couples affected by ADHD as well as from
more objective measures of behavior change (e.g., reports
from boss, observational coding) to rule out potential
biases.

Our pilot study explored the feasibility, acceptability,
and preliminary efficacy of the integrative group program
for ADHD in emerging adulthood. Although results were
not consistent across all measures, we generally found
positive effects of the program. Coupled with high rates of
participation and satisfaction, these preliminary results
were encouraging and suggest that treating emerging
adults with elevated ADHD symptoms in the context of
romantic relationships is a possible new direction for
future research on treatments for this population.
