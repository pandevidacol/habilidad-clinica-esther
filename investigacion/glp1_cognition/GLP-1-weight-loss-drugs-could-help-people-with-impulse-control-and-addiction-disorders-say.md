GLP-1 weight loss drugs could help people with impulse control and
addiction disorders, say researchers
Elisabeth Mahase
Glucagon-likepeptide-1receptoragonists(GLP-1RAs),
used to treat diabetes and for weight loss, may be
linked with a reduced risk of substance use and
neurocognitive disorders, a US study in people with
type 2 diabetes found, but could also carry an
increased risk of gastrointestinal disorders and drug
induced pancreatitis.1
Researchers from the Veterans Affairs St Louis
Healthcare System in Missouri used US Department
of Veterans Affairs databases to follow 1.96 million
people with type 2 diabetes for a median period of
3.7 years and to map associations between GLP-1RA
use and 175 health outcomes.
The study, published in Nature Medicine, included
215 970 people who used a GLP-1RA, comparing these
with 159 465 people who used sulfonylureas, 117 989
who used dipeptidyl peptidase IV (DPP4) inhibitors,
258 614 who used sodium glucose cotransporter-2
(SGLT2) inhibitors, and 1.2 million who used (“usual
care”) any non-GLP-1RA antihyperglycaemic drug.
Participants were enrolled between October 2017 and
December 2023.
The researchers reported that, in comparison with
the usual care group, use of GLP-1RAs was associated
with a reduced risk of substance related disorders,
including alcohol (hazard ratio (HR) 0.89 (95%
confidence interval 0.86 to 0.92)), cannabis (0.88
(0.83 to 0.93)), stimulants (0.84 (0.78 to 0.91)) and
opioids (0.87 (0.82 to 0.92)).
GLP-1RA use was also linked to reduced risk of
suicidal ideation or attempt or intentional self-harm
(HR 0.90 (0.86 to 0.94)), schizophrenia and other
psychotic disorders (0.82 (0.76 to 0.89)), and
Alzheimer’s disease (0.88 (0.78 to 0.99)).
