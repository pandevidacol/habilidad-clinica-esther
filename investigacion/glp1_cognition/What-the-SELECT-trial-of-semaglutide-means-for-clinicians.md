Naveed Sattar, Matthew M. Y. Lee & Darren K. McGuire
Accumulating data support the systemic 
benefits of semaglutide (and potentially 
other GLP-1RA-based therapies) in people 
with obesity, meaning that cardiologists 
and other clinicians must become familiar 
with prescribing them — particularly once 
market competition makes these drugs more 
accessible.
The SELECT trial — which evaluated semaglutide in individuals with obe-
sity and pre-existing atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (but with-
out diabetes) — was the first outcome trial of a glucagon-like peptide-1 
receptor agonist (GLP-1RA) in such a population, and its results were 
eagerly awaited. The primary outcome, reported in 2023, showed a 20% 
reduction in major cardiovascular outcomes with high-dose subcuta-
neous semaglutide (2.4 mg once a week) in the trial population1. The 
results of SELECT also showed that semaglutide nominally reduced 
all-cause mortality and incident heart failure, although the hierarchical 
testing scheme for secondary outcomes meant that it was not tested 
for superiority in regard to these conditions1. Even so, the accumulated 
data led the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) to approve a new 
indication for high-dose semaglutide — that is, to reduce cardiovascular 
risk in adults with obesity — making it the first medication licensed for 
obesity to get such an indication in the USA.
