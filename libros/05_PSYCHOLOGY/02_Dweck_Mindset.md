Introducing Mindset
CAROL S. DWECK, PH.D., A PROFESSOR AT STANDARD
UNIVERSITY boasts contributions to the ﬁeld and social psychology
are many and have garnered attention for their practical uses in
personal and public settings. Dweck’s “Mindset” earned her much
praise and response for being an innovative take on implicit theories of
intelligence. Personality, motivation, and development play key roles in
Dweck’s research and novel. “Mindset” has been used in businesses
and domestic settings, making it a commercial and academic success.
Many ﬁnd the mindset theory to be incredibly useful in their lives,
allowing them to cultivate and develop ﬂourishing interpersonal
relationship and positive outlooks on their successes and failures.
“Mindset” introduces the readers to psychological concepts that
oﬀer a new perspective on both stress and success. Dweck discusses
the diﬀerence between those who perceive their talents and abilities as
innate and those who believe hard work, learning and training have
resulted in their speciﬁc skill set. As the author explains, those who see
their capabilities as built rather than set in stone are better able to grow
and learn. Recognizing one’s mindset allows for personal growth and
learning. Dweck explains both mindsets in an attempt to help the
reader identify their own as well as others’ mindsets. The “ﬁxed”
mindset of believing in abilities one naturally has is harmful, Dweck
writes, because it places stress on the individual and fosters the idea
that failures are proof of a lack of skill. By contrast, those with “growth”
mindsets recognize that failing fosters learning and may help one
improve their abilities. Dweck’s discussion of those who believe they
are naturally talented versus those who perceive talent as ever-
changing is contextualized in terms of both individuals and larger
groups, such as communities. One example given is the diﬀerence
between praising a child for being smart and praising them for working
hard; according to the mindset theory, telling a child they are smart
would not foster as much growth or success as telling them they
worked hard. One method praises a quality as if it is inherent while the
other recognizes it as the result of work. By this virtue, one is more
likely to work and grow if they are told it is their work that led to their
level of skill instead of nature. This is clearly a valuable tool in

promoting positive mindsets in individuals, prompting them to achieve
success even when faced with partial or total failure.
Academics and non-academics alike have shown continued interest
in Dweck’s research and writing. “Mindset” has enjoyed a vast
readership and has been republished in an updated version to
accommodate current academic discourse. The author’s dedication to
contributing to psychology makes her a shining example of academic
support and research. Her work in both the academic and public
sphere makes “Mindset” a pleasurable and useful read. While it is not a
book marketed primarily as self-help, it still provides individuals and
businesses with the tools to better themselves. This makes it widely
popular and has helped it succeed across a variety of audiences.

Introducing the Author
CAROL S. DWECK WAS BORN OCTOBER 17, 1946. SHE HAS
TAUGHT at Columbia University, Harvard University, and the
University of Illinois. Her current teaching post is at Stanford University
Dweck graduated in 1967 from Barnard College and earned her Ph.D.
from Yale University in 1972. Most of Dweck’s professional career has
been marked by a clear desire to develop and research areas of
psychology that beneﬁt individuals in both private and public settings.
Many of Dweck’s contributions to psychology are mainly centered
around concepts of development in relation to personality and
motivation. The author’s dedication to science and psychology have
prompted her to publish for both academics and general readers. Her
work is an attempt to promote self-awareness and betterment through
scientiﬁc study and analysis. Much of Dweck’s research may be
applied to a wide range of audiences, making her work universally
relatable and commercially successful.
“Mindset” is not Dweck’s ﬁrst contribution to her ﬁeld. Her work
encompasses research and scholarly pieces which have been largely
regarded as vital to the ﬁeld of social psychology. Dweck’s book,
however, focused on implicit theories of intelligence- namely, the
mindset theory she developed and expanded over many years. As a
professor with prior involvement in the ﬁeld, the book was supported by
study and experience that made it well-rounded and scientiﬁcally
backed. Dweck’s colleagues supported many of the concepts put forth
in “Mindset” and oﬀered reviews and promotion that helped boost it into
the public sphere, where it has enjoyed success. The mindset theory
and its application from households to businesses makes it popular
amongst a variety of readers. “Mindset” functions as a tool for learning
and growth that helps the reader assess and reﬂect upon their
strengths and weaknesses. It is invaluable as a guide for self-
betterment as well as fostering success in others. The use of “Mindset”
in domestic and business settings makes it a versatile book with many
uses.
As of 2017, Dweck remains at Standford University, supporting and
contributing to her ﬁeld of work. She has expressed a desire to further
develop and study her mindset theory, honing it so that it may be used

more successfully in a variety of settings. “Mindset” continues to
provide academic and other readers with fascinating insights into
people and their ability to grow and learn. For many people, the novel
presents a chance to better themselves and their mindset. It is an
intellectual and motivational book, providing the chance to identify
personal failures and change if one so desires.

Discussion Questions
question 1
Dweck explains both the “ﬁxed” and “growth” mindsets. Do you agree
that there are only the two? Could someone ever possibly fall into both
categories? How so?


question 2
One of the target groups Dweck writes for is teachers and parents. Do
you think the book is helpful for these groups? Why or why not? Is it
possible to apply the mindset theories when dealing with children?


question 3
Many critics praised “Mindset” for its ability to reach the average
reader. Do you agree? Was the book an easy read? Did you have any
diﬃculty? Do you think there are groups Dweck is trying to reach that
would not be able to read the book?


question 4
Dweck’s book includes questions to help the reader identify what kind
of mindset they have. Were these questions well-structured? Do you
think they were successful in gauging your personality or mindset? Why
or why not?


question 5
Do you agree with the way the author describes “ﬁxed” mindset
individuals dealing with failure? Do you think that their reactions can be
entirely attributed to their mindset? Why or why not?


question 6
Dweck mentions that both genes and environmental factors aﬀect
individuals. Do you agree? Do you believe in one more than the other?
Why?


question 7
The author explains a revelation she had in which she realized there
were two mindsets when it comes to ability and learning. Do you think
her observance of students makes the belief more or less reputable?
Why or why not?


question 8
Much of “Mindset” encourages the reader to accept that they may not
be the best at what they do but can get better. Do you think it’s fair to
suggest that a simple change in view can revolutionize one’s life? Why
or why not? Is there anything else you think should be taken into
consideration?


question 9
Dweck stresses the importance of the mindset theory in the business
world. The author gives speciﬁc reasons for why managing people can
be diﬃcult and how it helps to apply mindset theory when doing so. Do
you think the author’s suggestions are relevant? Would they work in
real-world application?


question 10
There are “false” and “true” “growth” mindsets. As Dweck points out,
there are some that misinterpret the latter. Do you agree with the
author’s separation of true and false “growth” mindsets? Why or why
not? Are there clear lines or is it more of a spectrum?


question 11
One of the sections in “Mindset” deals with relationships. Do you think
mindsets are as important in relationships as the author says they are?
Why or why not? Would you identify mindsets in a relationship and try
to address them if necessary?


question 12
The “talent” mindset is explained in regards to Enron. What do you
think of Dweck’s inclusion of this example? Does it prove a point? Do
you think it makes sense, or do you disagree with the author’s
interpretation?


question 13
Dweck speciﬁcally talks about artistic ability and its relation to the
“ﬁxed” and “growth” mindsets. Do you agree with the ultimate
characterization of artistic ability? Why or why not?


question 14
Self-insight is important in “Mindset”, especially in the tests the author
provides. Do you think it’s fair to suggest some people are not unbiased
in appraising themselves? Why or why not?


question 15
Dweck discusses the dangers of praise and how negative labels can
work. In your opinion, is the author’s warning reasonable? Do you think
that the concepts in “Mindset” are true in regards to how children and
others should be prompted to succeed?


question 16
Dweck wrote a second book about the mindset theory six years after
“Mindset.” Why do you think she chose to write another book? Was it a
good idea? Why or why not?


question 17
The author has spent thirteen years teaching as a professor at
Stanford. Do you think her experience as a teacher gave her insight into
the mindset theory she discusses in the book?


question 18
“Mindset” has received much attention for being a pioneer in its ﬁeld. It
is also praised as being highly readable and relatable. Do you agree
with this reception? Why or why not?


question 19
Dweck’s book is meant to make the reader evaluate themselves and
learn how to be more successful based on psychological concepts. Do
you think the book does this well? Why or why not?


question 20
Some of “Mindset” involves Dweck’s belief that applying growth
mindsets to larger groups may help people grow as a whole. Do you
think that the mindset theory is applicable in large groups such as
businesses and schools? Why or why not?


question 21
Dweck is a professor at Stanford; she also holds a Ph.D. Do you think
this helped her writing in any way? How?


question 22
Dweck has other publications, some of which appeared before
“Mindset”. Why do you think this novel was so successful?


question 23
The author has explained her desire to enact change through her novel
and mindset theory. Why do you think she has such a passion for the
subject? Do you think she has a noticeable impact?


question 24
Do you agree with the author’s belief that people can become smarter
or more talented through hard work? Is there anything else you would
say impacts a person’s abilities?


question 25
The mindsets described in Dweck’s book are now widely discussed
and accepted in psychology. Do you think they are sound enough to
warrant discussion and research? Why or why not?


question 26
Dweck published a follow-up book six years after “Mindset”. Do you
think this was a reasonable time gap? Why or why not? Would you
have done diﬀerently?


question 27
Some of Dweck’s anecdotes include interviews with people who she
identiﬁes as having “ﬁxed” mindsets. Do you think the treatment of
these individuals was fair? Why or why not? Would you have done
diﬀerently?


question 28
Something that Dweck supports is an alternate form of supporting
children. Rather than praising a child’s ability, she stresses the
importance of praising the work done to achieve the ability. Do you
agree with this approach? Do you think it makes sense to apply
mindset theory when teaching children?


question 29
Businesses and people in social leadership positions are some of
Dweck’s target audiences. Do you think the mindset theory applies to
them? How? Would you have marketed the book to another speciﬁc
group? Why or why not?


question 30
Relationships are a small part of “Mindset”. Do you agree with the
author’s characterization of them and how mindset theory is involved?
Why or why not?
